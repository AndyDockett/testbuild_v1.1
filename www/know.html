Your digital tracks help drive the Internet economy. Having read this magazine, for example, you might receive more ads under the subject heading “statistics”. Here's a quick look at the economics of data on the Internet:

Free email services such as Gmail scan your inbox for keywords with an eye to targeted advertising. Google can conflate information about what you watch on YouTube with the messages in your Gmail account. In this way Google forms an image of you based on what you like and what you communicate about.

Parts of the travel industry have begun to show ads for more expensive hotels and trips to users who own a Mac computer, because the industry has discovered that Mac users spend 30 percent more on hotels than Windows users.

Persuasion profiling is all about targeting ads based on your state of mind. Businesses can form an idea of your

state of mind through search data — if you have searched for anti-depressive drugs, for example.

You don't have to post a bawdy photo from the office Christmas party on Facebook for someone to make a case for your infidelity. Christopher Soghoian, an American security and privacy researcher, explains:

“If a married couple is in the middle of a divorce, and one thinks the other is having an affair, then a lawyer's first step would be to get a court order and contact Facebook to learn who your friends are, how you exchange messages and if you log on from somebody else's home early in the morning.”

Some larger US insurance companies have introduced premiums that are based on your driving abilities and habits. The insurers keep motorists under total surveillance, and the better drivers get cheaper insurance.
